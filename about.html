Background & context

About the I Ching

History, practice, and a modern way to use the Book of Changes — without superstition or certainty.

What it is

The I Ching (Book of Changes) is one of the world’s oldest symbolic texts. It presents 64 hexagrams—patterns formed from six lines—used as a structured way to reflect on change, timing, and decision-making.

How casting works

A traditional method uses three coins to form one line at a time. After six lines, the full hexagram appears. Some lines are “changing,” suggesting movement—how a situation may evolve when conditions shift.

How to use it well

  • Bring a real question. Keep it practical and honest.
  • Read for structure. Treat the hexagram as an image of dynamics.
  • Pause before acting. Let insight settle before decisions.
  • Stay grounded. The value comes from reflection, not authority.

What this is not

Still Patterns is not predictive, prescriptive, or doctrinal. The I Ching is offered here as a contemplative tool — one way among many to clarify perception and widen perspective.

A quiet guideline
Use symbolic practice as you would a mirror: not to be told what to do, but to see more clearly.
